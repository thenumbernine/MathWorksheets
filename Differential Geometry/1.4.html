metric:
$g = e \cdot e$
$g_{ab} = e_a \cdot e_b$
metric inverse: $ [g^{ab}] = [g_{ab}]^{-1} $ such that $ g^{ac} g_{cb} = \delta^a_b $

Raising / lowering indexes:
$v^a = g^{ab} v_b$
$v_a = g_{ab} v^b$
Changing coordinates:
$v^I = {e_a}^I v^a$
$v_I = {e^a}_I v_a$

All metrics can be diagonalized into a signature metric.
Let $\eta_{IJ} = diag(signature)$, where $signature$ is the metric signature.
Let $\sigma$ be the product of all signatures.
Euclidian signature: 1, 1, 1; $\sigma = 1$
Lorentzian signature for particle physicists: +1, -1, -1, 1; $\sigma = -1$
Lorentzian signature for cosmologists: -1, +1, +1, +1; $\sigma = -1$

Metric determinant:
$det([g_{ab}]) = det( [{e_a}^I \eta_{IJ} {e_b}^J] )$
$det([g_{ab}]) = det( [{e_a}^I] )^2 det([\eta_{IJ}])$
$g = e^2 \sigma$
$e = \sqrt{|g|}$

Diagonalizing metrics:
Any $e_a$ can be transformed into an $e_I = {e^a}_I e_a$, such that $e_I \cdot e_J = \eta_{IJ}$, where $IJK$ is associated with the diagonalized metric coordinates.
In fact, if the metric is eigen-decomposed, then any number of $Q$ exist such that $Q V Q^{-1} = G$.
This means that, while the $IJK$ have a Minkowski signature, they may not necessarily be holonomic ... and therefore will have structure constants ...
Structure constants imply asymmetric connection on the 2nd and 3rd indexes, and a constant (Minkowski) metric as well implies antisymmetric on the 2nd and 3rd indexes.

$e^I = {e_u}^I dx^u$
$e_I = {e^u}_I \partial_u$
$e = det([{e_u}^I]) > 0$

Basis linear combination components:
$[ {e_u}^I ] \in GL(n, \mathbb{R})$

Inverse components:
$[ {e^u}_I ] = [ {e_u}^I ]^{-1}$

Linear combination orthogonality:
$e^I (e_J)$ $= {e_u}^I dx^u ({e^v}_J \partial_v)$
$= {e_u}^I {e^v}_J dx^u (\partial_v)$
$= {e_u}^I {e^v}_J \delta^u_v$
$= {e_u}^I {e^u}_J$
$= \delta^I_J$
