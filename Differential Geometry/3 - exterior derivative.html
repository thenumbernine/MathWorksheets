Wedge Product of Coordinate Basis One-Forms:

Wedge product of two one-form coordinate basis elements:
$dx^a \wedge dx^b = -dx^b \wedge dx^a$

Wedge product of the identical one-form coordinate basis element:
$dx^a \wedge dx^a = -dx^a \wedge dx^a$
Therefore $dx^a \wedge dx^a = 0$

Wedge product linearity:
$(\alpha dx^a + dx^b) \wedge dx^c = \alpha (dx^a \wedge dx^c) + dx^b \wedge dx^c$

Wedge product in terms of outer products:
$dx^a \wedge dx^b = dx^a \otimes dx^b - dx^b \otimes dx^a$

Generalize the definition of k wedge products to be:
$dx^{i_1} \wedge ... \wedge dx^{i_k} = k! dx^{[i_1} \otimes ... \otimes dx^{i_k]}$
Then we can see the degenerate k=2 case. Also we see associativity.

this discussion makes me tempted to define my wedge product as $dx^{i_1} \wedge ... \wedge dx^{i_k} = dx^{[i_1} \otimes ... \otimes dx^{i_k]}$, to make it similar to the antisymmetric index notation, and to keep the weight of $\wedge$ and $\otimes$ equal.

Therefore a (0 k) antisymmetric tensor in a coordinate basis has components:
$A = A_{i_1 ... i_k} dx^{i_1} \otimes ... \otimes dx^{i_k}$
$= A_{[i_1 ... i_k]} dx^{i_1} \otimes ... \otimes dx^{i_k}$
$= A_{i_1 ... i_k} dx^{[i_1} \otimes ... \otimes dx^{i_k]}$
$= \frac{1}{k!} A_{i_1 ... i_k} dx^{i_1} \wedge ... \wedge dx^{i_k}$

Wedge Product of Non-Coordinate Forms:

Wedge product antisymmetry on any one-form:
$a \wedge b$
$= \overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} (a_i dx^i) \wedge \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} (b_j dx^j)$
by wedge product linearity:
$= \overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} ((a_i dx^i) \wedge (b_j dx^j))$
$= \overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} (a_i b_j (dx^i \wedge dx^j))$
$= -\overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} (a_i b_j (dx^j \wedge dx^i))$
$= -\overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} ((b_j dx^j) \wedge (a_i dx^i))$
$= -b \wedge a$

Wedge product of two identical one-forms:
$a \wedge a$
$= \overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} (a_i dx^i) \wedge \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} (a_j dx^j)$
$= \overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} ((a_i dx^i) \wedge (a_j dx^j))$
$= \overset{n}{\underset{i=1}{\Sigma}} \overset{n}{\underset{j=1}{\Sigma}} (a_i a_j (dx^i \wedge dx^j))$
Notice that, for $i=j, dx^i \wedge dx^i = 0$, and for $i \ne j, dx^i \wedge dx^j = -dx^j \wedge dx^i$ are both summed, with identical coefficients $a_i a_j$,
Therefore we get:
$a \wedge a = 0$

Wedge product in terms of outer products:
$e^a \wedge e^b$
$= {e^a}_i dx^i \wedge {e^b}_j dx^j$
$= {e^a}_i {e^b}_j dx^i \wedge dx^j$
$= {e^a}_i {e^b}_j (dx^i \otimes dx^j - dx^j \otimes dx^i)$
$= ({e^a}_i dx^i \otimes {e^b}_j dx^j - {e^b}_j dx^j \otimes {e^a}_i dx^i)$
$= e^a \otimes e^b - e^b \otimes e^a$

Wedge product antisymmetry for p-form $a$ and q-form $b$:
$ a \wedge b $
$ = (a_{u_1 ... u_p} e^{u_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{u_p}) \wedge (b_{v_1 ... v_q} e^{v_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{v_q}) $
if any of $ u_1 ... u_p, v_q ... v_q $ are not unique then the result is zero (courtesy of $ e^u \wedge e^u = 0 $ ).
Otherwise:
$ = a_{u_1 ... u_p} b_{v_1 ... v_q} (e^{u_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{u_p}) \wedge (e^{v_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{v_q}) $
Rotate the first $ e^{v_1} $ to the far left side using $ e^u \wedge e^v = -e^v \wedge e^u $. Doing so requires $p$ applications of the rule.
$ = (-1)^p a_{u_1 ... u_p} b_{v_1 ... v_q} e^{v_1} \wedge (e^{u_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{u_p}) \wedge e^{v_2} \wedge ... \wedge e^{v_q} $
Continue to do the same for the rest of the $e^{v_k}$'s, doung so a number of $q$ times:
$ = (-1)^{pq} a_{u_1 ... u_p} b_{v_1 ... v_q} (e^{v_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{v_q}) \wedge (e^{u_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{u_p}) $
$ = (-1)^{pq} (b_{v_1 ... v_q} e^{v_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{v_q}) \wedge (a_{u_1 ... u_p} e^{u_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{u_p}) $
$ = (-1)^{pq} b \wedge a $

Notice that by this rule, for 0-form scalar a and 1-form w, this means:
$a w = a \wedge w = w \wedge a = w a$
This means the wedge product of scalars and k-forms can act interoperable with scalar multiplication of k-forms.

For one-forms, our basis is a 1-form $e^a$ and our scalar components $w_a$ are 0-forms, so this still works:
$w = w_a e^a = w_a \wedge e^a = e^a \wedge w_a$

Treat our vector basis $e_a$ as vector-valued 0-forms.
Then for scalar $\phi$ we have $e_a \wedge \phi = \phi \wedge e^a$
Therefore our scalar component representation of our vectors $v = v^a e_a$ can also be represented using wedge products:
$v = v^a \wedge e_a = e_a \wedge v^a$

Also notice that, if our vectors are considered vector-valued 0-forms, we get:
$e_a \wedge e_b = e_b \wedge e_a$
It seems that the wedge product is symmetric when used on vectors.
This would mean the $\otimes$ definition of wedge products, $e^{i_1} \wedge ... \wedge e^{i_k} = k! e^{[i_1} \otimes ... \otimes e^{i_k]}$ is only be true when applied to one-forms.
For vectors would it be $e_a \wedge e_b = e_a \otimes e_b$? Well, no, since $\otimes$ isn't symmetric.
Maybe $e_a \wedge e_b = e_{(a} \otimes e_{b)}$.
Or, to keep weighting consistent with the $\wedge$-vs-$\otimes$, should it be $e_a \wedge e_b = 2! e_{(a} \otimes e_{b)} = e_a \otimes e_b + e_b \otimes e_a$?

$ {d}({dw}) = {dd} ({w}_{a_1 ... a_p} {dx}^{a_1} \wedge ... \wedge {dx}^{a_p})$
$= {d} \partial_b ({w}_{a_1 ... a_p}) {dx}^b \wedge {dx}^{a_1} \wedge ... \wedge {dx}^{a_p}$
$= \partial_c \partial_b ({w}_{a_1 ... a_p}) {dx}^c \wedge {dx}^b \wedge {dx}^{a_1} \wedge ... \wedge {dx}^{a_p}$
Now we have the sum over the symmetric $\partial_c \partial_b$ and the antisymmetric ${dx}^b \wedge {dx}^c$, so our sum is zero.
${ddw} = 0$
